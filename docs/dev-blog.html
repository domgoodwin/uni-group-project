Progress is Key

12/01/2019

I am writing this final blog as our journey comes to an end and our game is released into the wild. Today’s blog will discuss the journey we (team Relaxed) have been on to make your haunted house mission possible.

This task begun with assigning roles depending on both skill and personal preference. Our team is built up of both technical and non-technical resources meaning task allocation was much easier. The more technical resources role (the developers) was to bring the mission to life and make your experience (the user) as fun as possible. Sated in my earlier blog “The bond we have as a team, and the approaches we have in place to deal with issues, gives me confidence that we are able to overcome any hurdles we may face” was indeed the case as it is the team moral and close bond we have which has helped up progress and complete this task with a game we are extremely proud of.

Post #8 - 07/01/2019 - Testing, Testing, 123...

Post #8

Testing, Testing, 123…

07/01/2019

As a team we have set our bar for quality very high. We do not want to release code into production, that users will end up using, that is riddled with bugs and glitches; no matter how big or how small. A game with higher quality will provide more fun and enjoyment for players and will make them want to come back and play again, and hopefully tell their friends and family to try it out as well. This would boost the player count, and in a real-world scenario would boost the value of the game to investors, backers and advertisers.

Therefore, in order to keep the quality bar high, we have routinely implemented our expansive and vigorous testing strategy. In addition to testing conducted automatically on builds and releases, as well as the automated unit tests created by our developers to check elements of the code, we have our regression testing pack and user acceptance testing procedures. Both of these suites of tests help us to not only check that the game continues to work correctly, but to ensure that no new additions go untested, as our testing packs are in a state of constant growth.

As we are fast approaching our final release, we are in the process of undertaking and implementing our testing strategy for possibly the last time, and therefore we want to make sure that the game has as many bugs in it as possible. Coming from a software development background we fully accept that no code is ever bug free, however we aim to spot and fix as many as we can find. This combined with our version control, peer reviewing, collaboration, and coding practices will hopefully result in a minimal amount of bugs being released into live; especially those that seriously impede the performance and playability of the game.

Each member of the team is now hard at work running through all our test scripts and scenarios, ensuring everything is A-Okay. Any bugs or issues that are found are documented on our Trello board, prioritised and then picked-up by a developer to implement a fix, if that is required. So far we have found 0 game-breaking bugs in our final release code – definitely something we can be proud of.

So then in summary myself and the rest of the team are very happy with the quality of work we have produced, and we are comfortable to be able to state that there are no game-breaking bugs being released in the final version; something that not many games developers are able to save – see Battlefield’s release history for starters…

We’ll be keeping up with the great work to make sure that when we release the final version on the 13th of January 2019, that we are just as certain of the game's quality then, as we are now.

Till then, keep playing and we hope, as always, that you continue to enjoy and play our game.

- Henry

Post #5 - 16/12/2018 - It's beginning to feel a lot like Christmas

Post #5

It’s begining to feel a lot like Christmas

16/12/2018

It’s full speed ahead for us for the next 4 weeks with the final release deadline set for the 13th of January. But, as we all know, between now and then we have Christmas and the New Year.

For most of us this is the first time in our software development careers where we have been in-charge of / in-control of a large-scale project and the management of said project, especially over the Christmas and New Year period.

At the beginning of the month, we setup our final Sprint, set to take us into early January, as per usual we pulled work in from the backlog, placing items into the “To do” section of our trello board, for members of the team to pick up and start working on.

Then, collectively, the work we brought into the final sprint would be released and form a part of the final version of the game; this all sounded great at the time.

However, as time drew closer to Christmas, we realised that we had bitten off more than we could chew. We had set ourselves too much work to do, and not factored into the fact that a lot of members of the team would be completely unavailable for x number of days over the next few weeks; and rightly so for that matter, everyone deserves some time to spend with their friends and family.

This meant that we had work in the Sprint to do, that we knew would not all get done, so, we therefore had to prioritise. A few days ago we set-up another team call where we went through and reviewed what we had planned for this sprint. Based upon priority, who was around, and the time it would take to implement and quality of work, we decided what we could drop out of the sprint and back onto the backlog; fully knowing that anything dropped back onto the backlog at this point in time was highly unlikely to be picked up before the final deadline, effectively meaning it would never be done - at least as not part of this university module.

This lead to some tough calls being made, and we ended up dropping out some of the “could” and “should” features, as detailed in our MoSCoW analysis; undertaken at the beginning of the project.

Whilst we had dropped out some of the features that we would have loved to implement, it did take off a lot of pressure of the team. Giving everyone more flexibility with their time, and allowed us to undertake the work we had kept in as part of the Sprint and complete it to a much higher standard - something that we did not want to compromise upon.

In the end, I believe that this was the best, and really, only option that we had. Had we chosen to leave everything in the Sprint it would have most likely ended in a disaster, causing stress and last minute panicking - something that we really wanted to avoid, the last thing any wants is to fragment the team at the last minute.

With that being said, we still have some exciting new features coming up, and some new additions too, so please keep racking up your high scores!

See you in the next blog.

Oh, and we hope you all have a wonderful festive period!

- Henry

Post #4 - 24/11/2018 - Different Modes

Post #4

Different Modes

24/11/2018

A key feature we wanted for the game was allowing for users to want to replay the game again and again. At the MVP stage this started as us speed running (trying to complete the game in the shortest time possible) the game against each other, aided by the points feature which is based on the seconds since you started the game.

Overtime this became repetitive and we realised all of the personal bests people set would be invalid if we made any significant changes to the game. This led us to add in challenges, difficulties and random mode. Challenge mode currently includes 1HP mode. This sets the players health to 1 and requires a perfect run through to finish.

Difficulties include normal and hard. Hard mode doubles the damage taken and some of the monsters in the game are doubled to increase the challenge.

Random mode switches up the positions in the game of all of the items to make it feel different each time.

Random mode was a difficult thing to add and a lot of the time spent was on the design and how to make sure it worked. Some of the ideas looked at included random whole room locations, multiples of each item and then random and fully generated houses however each of these caused problem; the main issue being how to guarantee the user can win the game.

The final method decided on was to randomise item locations in set, accessible locations. This meant things like the speed buff could be moved anywhere and it’s position is completely random, things like the strength buff and pickaxe could be anywhere not in the basement and the key would move only in the basement. This means the game has random locations and feels different but the key story points are still the same.

- Dom

Post #3 - 16/11/2018 - Team Building

Post #3

Team Building

16/11/2018

After our first call with Dr Evans, it became apparent that we weren't all on the same page, and we didn't all feel the same regarding our work and collaboration. This was something that obviously needed addressing, which we, as a team, took under our wings.

Having a good relationship with all members of the team not only allows for a better and more productive working environment, but also places an emphasis on good collaboration taking place; something that we definitely all want.

In order to improve the team dynamic we continued to do weather checks on our weekly calls, where we would go around and ask each member of the team to tell us how they are feeling about the project and workload in the form of a weather forecast. This was a fun and casual way for the team to express their feelings in a controlled way, and helped us to all understand how we, as a team, were feeling. From this we could see what we could do to improve the "weather", or, take a look at what we've done well and should continue to do for a sunny weather forecast.

We put aside 5 minutes at the beginning of the call to have a more personal catch-up with everyone, see what the team has been getting up to the week previous, and if they did anything fun or enjoyable that they'd like to share with the rest of the team. This allowed us to get to know everyone on a more personal level, which made it much easier to work together and collaborate on the project work.

At the end of the call, we spent the final 5 minutes all doing speed-runs of the game, to see who could complete the game the fastest and achieve the highest score out of the team responsible for making the game. This provided a competitive yet fun way to bring the call to an end, and too helped to boost interactions within the team during the call, thus boosting the morale as a whole.

We are now a much stronger team, and are working together much more closely, hopping on-and-off calls together, utilising group chats better, all with the aim of helping each other to produce the highest quality work and code that we, as a team, are capable of.

This is definitely something that we are going to continue doing, especially as we are able to look back and see how far we've come, and having experienced both "stormy" weather and now "sunny" weather, I think we can all agree we want to keep it all nice and sunny!

We hope you're continuing to play and enjoy the game, and we have some new and exciting features coming soon.

- Henry

Post #2 - 02/11/2018 - Issues, resolutions and teamwork

Post #2

Issues, resolutions and teamwork

02 / 11 / 2018

As I’m sure you are all aware, working as a team isn’t always smooth sailing especially when issues arise. In today’s blog I am going to discuss a number of issues we have faced throughout our haunted house mission and how we (team Relaxed) have overcome them.

Our first stumbling block arised pretty early on in this project, this was at submission two. Submission two included a video pitch, which describes what software we intend to produce during the project. This of course involved a number of decisions being made by the group, including game features and software choices. Issue one originated from a discussion to decide the best software for this task. The idea for a text based game was put forward by one team member which instantly caused a divide in opinions of the team. As agreed we approached this in a productive manner allowing each team member to put their idea forward eventually resulting in a final vote. A vote was cast and the decision was made to create the front end display using Javascript, Angular, Phaser and HTML 5. This will link to the backend using a Restful API and the back end of the game will be created using Spring (Java API) and Java. Given the outcome of this issue, and how quickly we were able to overcome this, we recognize the advantage of this approach and have applied this throughout the project.

Another common issue which has flagged up throughout, as expected, are bugs in the code. An example of a bug which was present in our hack, but was later resolved, was the sprite not disappearing after being ‘picked up’ by the user.

The example here shows the player and a pick axe. The pick axe should be attached to the player once they walk over it. The bug however recognises the player has picked up the pick axe, giving them the ability to use it, but the sprite in this case does not move. This has now been resolved. Our approach to bugs includes raising a ticket on our teams trello board, marking this task as a bug and tracking the progress until resolved. I’ve found this is extremely productive and it allows other teams members to pitch in by helping resolve one another's issues.

The bond we have as a team, and the approaches we have in place to deal with issues, gives me confidence that we are able to overcome any hurdles we may face.

Thank you for reading and I shall see you again next week (if we ever manage to escape!)

- Amy

Post #1 - 19/10/2018 - Teamwork makes the dream work

Post #1

Teamwork makes the dream work

19 / 10 / 2018

As I am sure you can all imagine, when we were assigned our team we were all a little apprehensive as to how the project would go as we had never met each other before let alone worked with each other. The first thing we did as a team was got to know a little bit about each other; what our hobbies are, what kind of role we are in and just general info about each other. We all found this experience revitalising as it gave us all confidence that we would be a great team. I must say, we are a super friendly bunch! In this blog, I will be discussing the journey we have been on so far.

After our first session, we all felt overwhelmed by the amount of different tasks that we had to complete for each submission. In an attempt to organise ourselves we set up various different organisational tools and a unified place to store all of our work. Through little discussion, we quickly all agreed to use Trello, Github, Slack and Google Drive. We have incorporated the Trello task board into our weekly meetings to ensure everyone is on track with their tasks. We all have found Trello extremely useful as a task management tool for a number of different reasons:

  1. Tasks are able to be assigned out to individuals
  2. Tasks can easily be updated
  3. Email Notifications when a task assigned to you is approaching its deadline
  4. It’s FREE!

As we are a team of eight; 6 developers, 2 business analysts we have split the workload accordingly. Myself and Amy have taken on the responsibility of project documentation and all non-techy tasks whilst Dom, Henry, Vishal, Colm, Allister and Tyler have all been working on the game.

One of our first submissions was to complete a Project Plan, at this moment in time we are currently in line with where expected to be at the beginning of the project. As we have to submit our MVP on Thursday 15th November we have had to strictly adhere to our plan in order to ensure all tasks have been completed.

Our developers have done a pretty good job of sticking to our plan and it has paid off as we have been able to add additional features into our game for the MVP than we initially planned during our pitch. This has made the MVP even more user friendly as there are less bugs in the game. From the progress that we have made so far, I think our game will be a great success and the audience is in for a thrill of their lives.

Thanks for reading!

- Chloe
